Why Most Account Structures Fail

Before we get into the solution, let's name the problems we see in nearly every audit. Understanding what's broken helps clarify why our structure is built the way it is.

Audience Cannibalization

This is the number one profitability killer. When your prospecting campaign and your retargeting campaign are both serving ads to the same person, you're bidding against yourself. Meta's auction system doesn't care that both campaigns belong to you. It treats them as separate advertisers competing for the same impression. The result: inflated CPMs, confused attribution, and the illusion that retargeting is "working" when it's really just stealing credit from prospecting.

Campaign Sprawl

Every time someone has a new idea, they spin up a new campaign. Birthday sale? New campaign. New product drop? New campaign. Testing a new creative angle? New campaign. Six months later you have 40 campaigns, and your learning data is scattered across all of them. Meta's algorithm needs roughly 50 conversions per ad set per week to exit the learning phase. Campaign sprawl makes that almost impossible.

No Measurement Foundation

If you can't answer the question "what is my blended customer acquisition cost by funnel stage," your account structure needs work. Most accounts we audit can't even tell you which audiences are actually incremental vs. which ones would have converted anyway. Structure isn't just about organization. It's the foundation of measurement.

The Wavy Studios Framework: Funnel-Based Architecture

Our account structure is built around a simple concept: campaigns are organized by the customer's relationship to your brand, not by product, promotion, or creative theme. This is the fundamental shift that changes everything.

We operate on three pillars. Every campaign in the account fits neatly into one of these, and exclusion logic between them ensures zero overlap.

Pillar 01 / Prospecting
Top of Funnel

Cold audiences. People who have never interacted with your brand. The goal is efficient reach and first-touch conversion at scale.

Broad / Open Targeting — No interests, fully algorithm-driven. This is where most of your budget lives at scale.
Interest Stacks — Layered interest combinations for brands still building their pixel data or in niche verticals.
Lookalike Audiences — 1-5% LALs seeded from your highest-value customer lists. Scale lever when broad starts to plateau.
Pillar 02 / Retargeting
Mid Funnel

Warm audiences. People who've engaged but haven't converted. The goal is to overcome objections and drive the purchase decision.

Website Visitors — Segmented by recency: 30, 60, 90, and 180-day windows give you control over frequency and messaging.
Engagers — IG/FB profile engagement audiences. High-intent social signals that convert well with social proof creative.
Video Viewers — 25/50/75/95% completion thresholds. Great for sequencing from awareness hooks to conversion CTAs.
Pillar 03 / Retention
Bottom of Funnel

Existing customers. People who've already purchased. The goal is to increase LTV through repeat purchases and upsells.

All Purchasers — Blanket retargeting to the full customer base with new product launches and seasonal promotions.
High-Value Customers — Segmented by purchase value or frequency. Premium messaging and exclusive offers.
Lapsed Customers — Win-back campaigns for customers who haven't purchased in 90+ days.

The magic is in the exclusions. Top of funnel explicitly excludes anyone who has visited your site in the last 30 days, engaged with your social content, or purchased from you—ever. Mid funnel excludes purchasers. Bottom funnel targets only purchasers. No one can exist in more than one pillar at a time.

Pro Tip: The 30-day exclusion window for website visitors in TOF campaigns is non-negotiable. It's the single most important audience exclusion in your entire account. Without it, you're paying acquisition CPMs for retargeting traffic.

Meta Account Structure Breakdown

Let's get into the tactical execution. Here's exactly how we structure Meta accounts following the framework above.

Campaign Architecture

We run between 6-12 campaigns total, depending on spend level. Each campaign has a specific job in the funnel and uses Advantage+ Campaign Budget (formerly CBO) to let Meta allocate spend dynamically across ad sets.

TOF_BROAD_PROS_PURCH TOF_INT_PROS_PURCH TOF_LAL_PROS_PURCH MOF_WEB_RET_PURCH MOF_ENG_RET_PURCH BOF_CUST_RET_PURCH

That's the base structure. Here's what each campaign does:

Top of Funnel Campaigns

TOF_BROAD_PROS_PURCH — This is your workhorse. Broad targeting, no interests, optimized for purchase. Exclusions: 30-day website visitors, 365-day engagers, lifetime purchasers. Budget: 60-70% of total spend at scale.

TOF_INT_PROS_PURCH — Interest-based prospecting for brands that need more control or are in niche verticals where broad struggles. Same exclusions as BROAD. Budget: 10-15% when needed, often paused once broad performs.

TOF_LAL_PROS_PURCH — Lookalike audiences seeded from highest-value customer segments. Used as a scaling lever when broad campaigns hit efficiency ceilings. Same exclusions. Budget: 15-20% when active.

Mid Funnel Campaigns

MOF_WEB_RET_PURCH — Website visitors in the last 30/60/90/180 days, excluding purchasers. This is where you test different messaging for different engagement levels. Budget: 15-20% of total spend.

MOF_ENG_RET_PURCH — Social engagers (IG/FB profile visits, post likes, video views). High-intent signals. Exclude purchasers. Budget: 5-10%.

Bottom of Funnel Campaigns

BOF_CUST_RET_PURCH — All customers, optimized for repeat purchase. New product drops, restocks, seasonal promotions. Budget: 5-10%.

Warning: Don't run separate campaigns for every product category or creative theme. That's campaign sprawl. Use ad set-level organization within these campaigns, or consolidate creative testing into a single TOF campaign with DCT (Dynamic Creative Testing).

Google Ads Structure

Google follows the same funnel-based philosophy, but the tactics differ because the intent signals are different.

Search Campaigns

Search campaigns are inherently bottom-funnel. People searching for your brand name or category keywords already have intent. The structure is simple:

SRCH_BRAND_PURCH SRCH_COMP_PURCH SRCH_CAT_PURCH

SRCH_BRAND_PURCH — Brand name and misspellings. Exact match. Protect your brand traffic from competitors. Budget: Uncapped—never lose a branded search.

SRCH_COMP_PURCH — Competitor brand names. Phrase match. Use if you're in a competitive vertical and can steal share of voice. Budget: 10-15% of search budget.

SRCH_CAT_PURCH — Category keywords (e.g., "best running shoes," "affordable CRM software"). Broad match with smart bidding. Budget: 20-30% of search budget. This is discovery traffic.

Performance Max (PMAX)

PMAX is Google's AI-driven black box campaign. It's effective but requires structure to prevent it from cannibalizing your brand search traffic.

PMAX_PROS_PURCH PMAX_RET_PURCH

PMAX_PROS_PURCH — New customer acquisition. Exclude all customer lists. Feed it product catalog, creative assets, and let it run across Shopping, Display, YouTube, Search, Discover. Budget: 30-40% of total Google budget.

PMAX_RET_PURCH — Customer retargeting. Target purchasers only. Use for upsells and repeat purchases. Budget: 10-15%.

Critical Setup: Add your brand keywords as negative keywords in PMAX campaigns. Otherwise, PMAX will bid on your brand terms and cannibalize your cheaper branded search traffic. This is a common mistake that wastes thousands per month.

YouTube Campaigns

YouTube gets its own campaign structure because video requires different creative and longer consideration windows.

YT_PROS_VIEWS YT_RET_PURCH

YT_PROS_VIEWS — Cold prospecting optimized for video views. Affinity and in-market audiences. Goal: Top-of-funnel awareness at scale. Budget: 10-15% of Google budget.

YT_RET_PURCH — Retargeting video viewers and website visitors with direct-response creative. Optimized for conversions. Budget: 5-10%.

Naming Convention System

Naming conventions sound boring. They're not. They're the difference between an account you can report on instantly and an account that requires 30 minutes of mental archaeology every time someone asks "how's the account performing?"

Our naming system follows this structure:

STAGE_AUDIENCE_TACTIC_OBJECTIVE Examples: TOF_BROAD_PROS_PURCH MOF_WEB30_RET_PURCH BOF_HVCU_RET_PURCH

Component Breakdown

STAGE — Where in the funnel. TOF (Top), MOF (Mid), BOF (Bottom). This tells you immediately if it's prospecting, retargeting, or retention.

AUDIENCE — Who you're targeting. BROAD (no targeting), INT (interest-based), LAL (lookalike), WEB30 (30-day website visitors), ENG (engagers), CUST (customers), HVCU (high-value customers).

TACTIC — What you're doing. PROS (prospecting), RET (retargeting). Descriptive, not prescriptive.

OBJECTIVE — What you're optimizing for. PURCH (purchases), VIEWS (video views), LEADS (lead generation), ATC (add to cart).

Consistency Wins: The exact convention doesn't matter as much as using it consistently. When everyone on your team knows how to read campaign names at a glance, decision-making speeds up and errors drop.

Budget Allocation Strategy

Here's how we allocate budget across the funnel at different spend levels. These are starting points, not gospel. Let the data guide adjustments.

Spend Level TOF % MOF % BOF % Notes
$30K-$50K/mo 60% 30% 10% Focus on acquisition, limited retention budget
$50K-$150K/mo 65% 25% 10% Shift to TOF as retargeting pool saturates
$150K-$300K/mo 70% 20% 10% Heavy TOF investment to maintain growth
$300K+/mo 75% 15% 10% Mature accounts need constant TOF pressure

The pattern: as you scale, TOF becomes more important. Retargeting pools are finite. You can't scale a business on retargeting alone. New customer acquisition must increase proportionally.

The Retargeting Trap: Early-stage brands often over-index on retargeting because the ROAS looks incredible. Don't be fooled. High retargeting ROAS is often a mirage—those customers would have converted anyway. You're not adding incremental revenue, you're just taking credit for it.

Measurement Framework

Structure without measurement is just organization theater. Here's how we actually measure performance and make decisions.

Daily Metrics Dashboard

  • Blended CAC by Funnel Stage — What does it cost to acquire a customer from TOF, MOF, and BOF independently?
  • New Customer Rate — What % of purchases are first-time customers? If this drops, your acquisition campaigns are broken.
  • Budget Pacing vs. Target — Are campaigns hitting their daily budgets or underspending? Underspending = missed opportunity.
  • Frequency by Campaign — If frequency > 2.5 in prospecting campaigns, you're burning out your audience.

Weekly Performance Review

  • CPM Trends — Rising CPMs = auction saturation or creative fatigue. Investigate immediately.
  • Landing Page CVR — Stable traffic but dropping conversion rate? The issue isn't media, it's site experience.
  • Incrementality Check — Run holdout tests quarterly to measure how much revenue you'd still generate if you turned off retargeting.

The key insight: don't optimize for ROAS. Optimize for profit contribution. A campaign with 3x ROAS spending $10K/mo is less valuable than a campaign with 2.5x ROAS spending $100K/mo.

Monthly Ritual: Export all campaign data. Calculate profit margin after CAC. Rank campaigns by total profit dollars, not ROAS. Kill the bottom 20%. Reallocate that budget to the top 20%. Repeat every 30 days.

Migration Plan: Moving from Chaos to Structure

You can't just delete 40 campaigns and start fresh. Meta's algorithm needs learning data, and turning everything off resets the pixel to zero. Here's how to migrate without nuking your performance.

Phase 1: Audit & Map (Week 1)

  • Export all campaign data from the last 90 days.
  • Categorize every campaign into TOF, MOF, or BOF based on targeting.
  • Identify campaigns with overlapping audiences (this is most of them).
  • Document current spend allocation by funnel stage.

Phase 2: Build New Structure (Week 2)

  • Create the new TOF/MOF/BOF campaigns following the framework above.
  • Set budgets to match current allocation (don't change spend yet).
  • Apply all audience exclusions to prevent overlap.
  • Migrate your best-performing creative into the new campaigns.

Phase 3: Parallel Testing (Week 3-4)

  • Run old and new structures simultaneously at 50% budget each.
  • Monitor performance daily. The new structure may underperform initially due to learning phase.
  • If new structure is within 15% of old structure efficiency by week 4, proceed to phase 4.

Phase 4: Full Migration (Week 5)

  • Shift 100% of budget to the new structure.
  • Pause (don't delete) old campaigns. Keep them for 30 days as a rollback option.
  • Monitor closely for the first 7 days. Expect turbulence as the pixel recalibrates.

Phase 5: Optimization (Week 6+)

  • Let the new structure run for at least 2 weeks before making changes.
  • Start A/B testing creative, offers, and landing pages within the structure.
  • Adjust budget allocation based on profit contribution, not ROAS.

Patience Required: Performance may dip 10-20% during migration due to the learning phase. This is normal. The algorithm needs time to recalibrate. Don't panic and revert to the old structure after 3 days. Give it at least 14 days.
