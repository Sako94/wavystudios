How We Produce 120+ Creatives Per Month

The brief template, creator network, and production workflow behind high-velocity creative operations. No magic—just systems.

Every week, brands ask us the same question: "How do you produce so much creative without sacrificing quality?"

The answer isn't more designers. It's not a bigger budget. It's not AI (though we use it). The answer is systems.

We've built a creative production machine that outputs 120+ ad variations per month for our clients—with a team of 3 designers and a network of 50+ UGC creators. Here's exactly how it works.

The Math Behind High-Velocity Creative

First, let's break down what "120+ creatives" actually means:

  • 30 net-new concepts: Completely fresh angles, hooks, and creative directions
  • 60 iterations: Variations on winning concepts (new hooks, CTAs, formats)
  • 30 format adaptations: Resizing, reformatting for different placements

This isn't about producing garbage at scale. It's about systematic iteration on what works while continuously testing new angles.

The Weekly Creative Sprint

Everything runs on a weekly sprint cycle. No monthly planning marathons. No quarterly creative refreshes. Every week, the machine runs:

Mon

Performance Review

Analyze last week's winners and losers

Tue

Brief Development

Write briefs for new concepts + iterations

Wed

Production

Designers + creators execute briefs

Thu

Review & Revise

QA, feedback, final edits

Fri

Launch

New creative goes live

This cadence creates forcing functions. No brief sits in limbo. No creative waits for "final approval." The rhythm keeps everything moving.

The Brief Template That Makes It Possible

Speed comes from clarity. Every brief we write follows the same 7-element structure:

The 7-Element Creative Brief

  1. Angle: The core message or positioning we're testing (1 sentence max)
  2. Hook: The first 3 seconds/first line that stops the scroll
  3. Proof Points: 2-3 specific claims, stats, or social proof to include
  4. CTA: The exact action we want viewers to take
  5. Format: Static, video, carousel, UGC, etc.
  6. Reference: 1-2 examples of what we're going for
  7. Success Metric: What "winning" looks like for this creative

That's it. No 10-page creative strategy documents. No mood boards. No "creative exploration." A brief that can be written in 10 minutes and executed in 2 hours.

The Test: If a designer or creator needs to ask clarifying questions before starting, the brief failed. Rewrite it.

The 70/30 Split: Evergreen vs. Experimental

Not all creative is created equal. We split production into two buckets:

70% Evergreen (Iterations on Winners)

These are variations on concepts that have already proven themselves. The angle works—we're just testing new executions:

  • New hooks on the same angle
  • Different CTAs
  • Format changes (static → video, video → carousel)
  • New proof points or social proof
  • Creator/talent swaps

30% Experimental (Net-New Concepts)

These are fresh angles we haven't tested before. Higher risk, higher potential reward:

  • New positioning or messaging angles
  • Different target personas
  • Format experiments
  • Competitive angles
  • "Weird" ideas from the team

This ratio ensures we're always improving on what works while continuously discovering new winners.

The UGC Creator Network

UGC is our highest-performing format across almost every account. But managing creators is a nightmare—unless you systematize it.

How We Built a 50+ Creator Network

  1. Source continuously: We're always recruiting, not just when we need content. Billo, Insense, and direct outreach on TikTok/Instagram.
  2. Test before committing: Every new creator gets a single paid test brief. If they deliver, they join the network. If not, we move on.
  3. Categorize by strength: Some creators are great at testimonials. Others nail product demos. We tag everyone by what they're best at.
  4. Pay fast, pay fairly: Net-7 payment terms, always. $150-400 per video depending on complexity and usage rights.

The Creator Brief

Creator briefs are different from design briefs. They need to be more prescriptive on format while leaving room for authentic delivery:

UGC Creator Brief Template

  • Hook Script: Exact first line they need to say
  • Talking Points: 3-4 bullet points to hit (not a full script)
  • B-Roll List: Specific shots we need (product in hand, using product, etc.)
  • Length: Target duration (usually 15-45 seconds)
  • Don'ts: Common mistakes to avoid (music, filters, etc.)
  • References: 2-3 example videos showing the style we want

We never send full scripts. The content comes out robotic. Talking points + examples = authentic delivery with on-message content.

The Tech Stack

Tools matter less than process, but here's what we use:

  • Foreplay: Ad library and swipe file—competitive research and inspiration
  • Figma: Design production for static and motion graphics
  • Frame.io: Video review and approval workflow
  • Notion: Brief database and creative archive
  • Slack: Creator communication (faster than email)
  • Google Sheets: Performance tracking and winner/loser analysis

The stack isn't special. The workflows built on top of it are.

The Review Process That Doesn't Kill Speed

Most creative operations die in the review process. Feedback loops stretch for days. Stakeholders pile on contradictory notes. By the time creative launches, the moment has passed.

Our review process has two rules:

  1. One reviewer per creative type. Static gets reviewed by the design lead. Video by the creative strategist. UGC by the media buyer who'll run it. No committees.
  2. 24-hour turnaround max. If feedback isn't delivered within 24 hours, the creative launches as-is. This sounds scary. It works.

The result: creative moves from brief to live in 72 hours or less.

What This Looks Like in Practice

Here's an actual week from one of our fitness apparel accounts:

  • Monday: Reviewed last week's tests. 2 winners (scale immediately), 3 losers (kill), 5 inconclusive (need more spend)
  • Tuesday: Wrote 8 briefs—4 iterations on winners, 4 new angle tests
  • Wednesday: Designers produced 12 static variations. Sent 4 briefs to UGC creators.
  • Thursday: Reviewed and approved 10 statics. Received 3 UGC videos, requested 1 revision.
  • Friday: Launched 13 new creatives. Archived all briefs and assets.

Multiply this by 4 weeks, add the UGC that comes in on rolling deadlines, and you hit 120+ per month without breaking a sweat.

The Metrics That Matter

Volume is meaningless without quality. We track three metrics religiously:

  1. Win Rate: What percentage of new concepts become scale-worthy winners? Target: 15-20%
  2. Iteration Efficiency: How many iterations does it take to find the best version of a winner? Target: 3-4
  3. Time to Scale: How quickly do winners get scaled to full budget? Target: 48 hours

If win rate drops, we're testing bad angles. If iteration efficiency drops, our briefs aren't specific enough. If time to scale drops, our review process is broken.

Start Building Your System

You don't need 120 creatives per month. You need a system that can produce whatever volume your account requires—consistently, predictably, without heroics.

Start here:

  1. Adopt the 7-element brief template. Use it for every creative, no exceptions.
  2. Implement the weekly sprint cadence. Even if you're producing 20 creatives a month, the rhythm creates momentum.
  3. Build your creator network—even 5 reliable creators changes the game.
  4. Track win rate, not just volume. Output means nothing if it doesn't convert.

The system scales. The question is whether you'll build it.
